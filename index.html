The Great Gray Lady

Somewhere in the mountains, the great gray lady waits for me, on a dark and lonely runway. She is not a thing built, but a thing summoned, an oath given shape. A hush laid down like a spell— first to deceive, then to deny, then to belong, draped in the color of storm clouds that have forgotten how to rain. I do not come to her as the riders do, with their maps and their urgent demands. They see a chariot. I see the burden; the price of always knowing. She carries the long, flat crown upon her spine, that terrible, unblinking third eye which sees through the skin of the world. It is a weight no creature should bear, a gift no creature asked for, the ability to know the truth of what hides in the dark. In her shadow I am small and warm against her absolute cold. I am not a master. I am a keeper. I know the language of her veins— copper and light— threaded through her like a second nervous system, finer than nerves, harsher than bone. I know where she is fragile. I know the cost of her vision. The others will come soon enough. They will wake her with fire, they will demand she scream, they will spur her into the thinning air to hunt for ghosts. Those that have never been, and must never be. But at this hour, she belongs only to the quiet. And I am the only one who understands she does not want to hunt. She wants only to watch. I place my hand on her flank, a promise between the maker and the made: not to ask for miracles without knowing the price paid. Rest now, I tell her, before the noise begins. Rest, while the world is still allowed to hide. Rest, while the blindness lasts.

Project I — FPGA FMCW Radar DSP Core

Completed real-time baseband core that turns raw I/Q beat samples into detections and tracks.

What it does

This is a hardware-oriented DSP pipeline for an FMCW radar receiver. Per frame, it ingests complex I/Q samples (fast-time within a chirp, slow-time across chirps), produces a calibrated range–Doppler power map, performs CFAR detection, and maintains Track-While-Scan (TWS) tracks.

Processing chain (stage-by-stage)

  1. Hamming window (fast-time) — suppresses range sidelobes prior to FFT.
  2. Range FFT (“pulse compression”) — converts beat frequency bins to range bins per chirp.
  3. Corner turn (matrix transpose) — reorders memory from [chirp][range] to [range][chirp] for Doppler processing.
  4. MTI Doppler notch (3‑pulse canceller) — removes near-zero Doppler clutter along slow-time.
  5. Doppler window (slow-time) — reduces Doppler sidelobes prior to coherent integration.
  6. Coherent Doppler integration — integrates energy coherently across chirps (typically via Doppler FFT or equivalent accumulation).
  7. Magnitude approximation (α‑max/β‑min) — low-cost |I+jQ| estimate for downstream detection.
  8. 2D Ordered-Statistic CFAR — adaptive thresholding in range–Doppler with robust noise estimation.
  9. TWS with α‑β filtering — track management + smoothing of range/Doppler (and optionally angle) measurements.

Key algorithms (implementation intent)

1) Hamming window sidelobe suppression

The fast-time samples of each chirp are multiplied by a Hamming window before the range FFT to reduce spectral leakage and suppress range sidelobes. In fixed-point hardware this is typically implemented as a coefficient ROM + complex multiply (or a real multiply applied identically to I and Q).

2) Range FFT pulse compression

FMCW “pulse compression” is achieved by FFTing the de-chirped beat signal. Each FFT bin corresponds to a beat frequency, mapped to range by the radar’s chirp slope. The output is a complex range profile per chirp, suitable for slow-time processing.

3) Corner turning (matrix transposition)

Doppler processing operates across chirps for each range bin, while acquisition naturally arrives as contiguous fast-time samples. The core performs a corner turn so the same data can be streamed efficiently into the slow-time stages: conceptually, it transposes a range–chirp matrix using ping‑pong buffering / BRAM banking to sustain throughput.

4) MTI Doppler notch filtering (3‑pulse canceller)

A 3‑pulse canceller is applied along slow-time to notch out stationary/near‑stationary clutter. A common form is: y[k] = x[k] − 2x[k−1] + x[k−2]. This creates a Doppler-domain zero at DC and improves detection of moving targets in the presence of strong clutter.

5) Doppler windowing + 6) coherent Doppler integration

After MTI, a slow-time window (e.g., Hamming/Hann/Blackman family) reduces Doppler sidelobes. Coherent integration then accumulates complex energy across chirps. In practice this is most often implemented as a Doppler FFT per range bin (yielding a full range–Doppler map), but the integration block is structured so that the windowed slow-time vector is the unit of work, matching FPGA-friendly streaming.

7) α‑max/β‑min magnitude approximation

To avoid an expensive square‑root, the magnitude of a complex bin is approximated using a weighted max/min of the absolute components: |z| ≈ α·max(|I|,|Q|) + β·min(|I|,|Q|). This provides a hardware‑efficient, monotonic proxy for power/energy used by CFAR and tracking.

8) 2D Ordered‑Statistic CFAR (OS‑CFAR)

OS‑CFAR forms an adaptive threshold per cell-under-test by collecting a 2D training ring in range–Doppler, sorting (or partially selecting) the samples, and choosing an order statistic (e.g., the k-th smallest) as the noise estimate. This is robust against interfering targets compared to CA‑CFAR. Guard cells protect the CUT from target leakage; scaling sets the desired false‑alarm rate.

9) Track‑While‑Scan with α‑β filtered TWS

Detections are associated into tracks, with α‑β filtering providing a lightweight constant‑velocity estimator: the α term corrects position (e.g., range bin), and β corrects velocity (e.g., Doppler bin / radial speed). Tracks are confirmed, maintained, and pruned using hit/miss logic, enabling continuous scan‑to‑scan tracking.

Outputs

  • Range–Doppler map suitable for visualization, logging, or downstream fusion.
  • Detection list (range bin, Doppler bin, magnitude/score) from 2D OS‑CFAR.
  • Track list (TWS) with α‑β filtered state estimates and track quality.

About

A few things I like, and the threads I keep pulling.

Aurellia Wright

I worked in IT through high school, then spent three years as an AMHS engineer at GlobalWafers America — their 300mm silicon wafer fab in Sherman, Texas. I designed and maintained the automated material handling systems that move wafer carriers between process tools. It was sophisticated work in a demanding environment, and it taught me how real systems fail and how they're kept running.

Eventually I wanted harder problems. I went back to high school, earned a 4.0, and used it to get into KTH Royal Institute of Technology on the Engineering Physics track.

Somewhere along the way I fell into a deep fascination with surface-to-air missile systems and the war over the electromagnetic spectrum. The FPGA and radar work came out of that — I needed to understand how these systems actually see, so I started building one.

This site is where I keep track of what I'm building. I also spend time on the other side of the brain — percussion, poetry, and whatever book has me cornered.
